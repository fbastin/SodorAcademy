Chapter One

A White Morning

The Island of Sodor woke one morning wrapped in a thick, grey blanket of fog. Sir Topham Hatt stood on the platform at Tidmouth Sheds, his top hat disappearing into the mist above his shoulders.

"Be careful today, engines," he warned, hands clasped behind his back. "The fog is thick, and the passengers are counting on you to be Really Useful."

Thomas puffed out his chest — or rather, his boiler. "Don't worry, sir! I know every bend of my branch line. I could run it with my eyes closed!"

Gordon, who was idling nearby with the kind of dignity only a large express engine can manage, gave a long, slow toot. "That," he said, "is precisely what worries us."

Chapter Two

Into the White

Thomas set off into the white morning, his blue paint swallowed almost immediately by the fog. His headlamp threw a small golden cone of light ahead, but beyond it, the world had simply vanished.

At first, Thomas enjoyed the mystery of it. The familiar fields were gone. The old stone bridge appeared suddenly, like a magic trick, and disappeared just as quickly.

Every station materialized from nothing and then dissolved back into the grey.

But then, near Maithwaite, Thomas heard something unusual.

A sound — small and thin — drifting through the fog.

Baaaa.

Chapter Three

A Most Inconvenient Lamb

Thomas applied his brakes with a hiss and a screech, rolling to a gentle stop. There, sitting calmly in the middle of the track, was a lamb. It blinked at Thomas with enormous amber eyes, entirely unbothered.

"Oh dear," said Thomas.

The lamb said nothing useful.

Thomas couldn't reverse — Annie and Clarabel were behind him, full of passengers. He couldn't go forward. He blew his whistle, long and sharp, hoping the farmer might hear. The fog swallowed the sound whole.

It was his fireman, Burt, who climbed down and gently scooped the lamb off the tracks, carrying it to the grassy bank where, presumably, it belonged.

The lamb watched them go with those same calm, amber eyes, as though it had merely been resting between important appointments.

Chapter Four

The Girl in the Yellow Raincoat

Thomas arrived at the harbour twelve minutes late. He felt terrible about it — truly, deeply terrible, in the way that only Thomas can.

But waiting on the platform was a small girl in a yellow raincoat, her face pressed against the foggy air.

"You came!" she said, when Thomas pulled in.

"Of course," said Thomas. "I'm always here."

"Daddy said the trains might not run in the fog."

"Fog," said Thomas, with great seriousness, "has never stopped me yet."

The girl smiled, and ran to find her mother, and Thomas felt something warm settle in his boiler — warmer than steam, warmer than coal.

"By afternoon, the fog had burned away. Sodor gleamed green and gold in the autumn sun."

The End

That evening, Gordon asked if the journey had gone smoothly.


"Perfectly," said Thomas.


He did not mention the lamb. Some things, he decided, are best kept between an engine and his branch line.
